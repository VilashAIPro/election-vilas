02 — Timeline

Election timeline, step by step

Click each step to expand the full details. Track your progress below.

0 of 8 steps explored
01Election announcedMonths before▾
The Election Commission of India (ECI) announces the election schedule including polling dates, counting date, and key deadlines. The Model Code of Conduct (MCC) comes into force immediately — the government cannot announce new welfare schemes or use state machinery for campaigns from this moment.
02Voter registrationOngoing▾
Citizens 18+ can register using Form 6 online at voters.eci.gov.in or at their Electoral Registration Officer (ERO). A photo Voter ID card (EPIC) is issued. Voters can verify their names on the electoral roll at electoralsearch.in or by calling 1950. Name corrections use Form 8.
03Candidate nominationsWeeks before▾
Candidates file nomination papers with the Returning Officer. A security deposit of ₹25,000 (General) or ₹12,500 (SC/ST) is required for Lok Sabha. Candidates must disclose criminal records, assets, and educational qualifications publicly. Scrutiny and withdrawal deadlines follow.
04Campaign periodWeeks before▾
Parties hold rallies, debates, and run ads. Campaign expenditure is capped at ₹95 lakh per candidate for Lok Sabha in most states. A 48-hour campaign silence begins before polling. Exit polls cannot be broadcast until all phases across India conclude.
05Polling day — votingElection day▾
Polling stations open 7 AM to 6 PM. Voters present EPIC or an approved alternate ID. Votes are cast on EVMs — a VVPAT slip is printed for 7 seconds confirming the voter's choice. Security forces (typically CRPF) are deployed. Multi-phase polls spread voting over several days across different states.
06Polls close — EVMs sealedEnd of polling▾
After the last voter votes, EVMs and VVPAT machines are sealed. They are transported to strong rooms under armed guard. Candidate agents receive mock poll slips as receipts. Multi-layer physical and digital security ensures tamper-proof storage until counting day.
07Counting day and resultsDesignated day▾
EVMs are opened at counting centres under supervision. Counting agents from each party observe. Results are transmitted to ECI in real time. The candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins (First-Past-The-Post). NOTA results are also announced. Candidates may request recounts.
08Government formationDays after results▾
The President invites the party or coalition with 272+ seats to form the government. Coalition talks may precede this in a hung parliament. The Prime Minister and Cabinet are sworn in. The outgoing government remains as caretaker until swearing-in is complete.

India-specific rules

Model Code of Conduct (MCC)
Activated immediately when election dates are announced. Restricts the government from announcing new welfare schemes, making official transfers, or using state funds/machinery for campaigning. Violating the MCC can lead to show-cause notices from ECI.
Alternate voter ID (12 documents accepted)
If you don't have an EPIC card, you may use: Aadhaar, Passport, Driving Licence, MNREGA Job Card, Bank/Post Office Passbook with photo, Smart Card issued by Ministry of Labour, CGHS/ECHS photo card, Pension document with photo, or official identity document issued by Government of India/State.
Campaign expenditure limits
Lok Sabha: ₹95 lakh per candidate (larger states), ₹75 lakh (smaller states/UTs). State Assembly: ₹40 lakh per candidate (larger states). Expenses are tracked by ECI-appointed expenditure observers. Violation can lead to disqualification under RPA 1951.
Anti-defection law (10th Schedule)
MPs and MLAs who switch parties or vote against their party whip without sanction can be disqualified. The Speaker (Lok Sabha) or Chairman (Rajya Sabha) adjudicates defection cases. This was introduced by the 52nd Constitutional Amendment in 1985.
VVPAT verification
Since 2019, every EVM is paired with a Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail machine. After you press the EVM button, a paper slip appears behind a glass screen for 7 seconds showing your candidate's name and symbol before dropping into a sealed box. This adds a physical audit trail to electronic voting.
Paid news prohibition
Publishing or broadcasting any news or article for payment without disclosure as an advertisement is prohibited and treated as a corrupt practice under the Representation of the People Act. ECI monitors media during elections through Media Certification and Monitoring Committees (MCMCs).

05 — Types

Types of elections in India

India holds several distinct types of elections across national, state, and local levels.

National

General Election (Lok Sabha)

Elects 543 MPs every 5 years. Determines which party or coalition forms the Central Government and who becomes Prime Minister.

State

State Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

Each state elects its legislative assembly. The majority party forms the state government led by the Chief Minister. Timing varies by state.

Upper House

Rajya Sabha Election

Members are indirectly elected by state legislative assemblies using single transferable vote. One-third retire every two years. Citizens do not vote directly.

Local Body

Panchayat / Municipal

Elections for gram panchayats, panchayat samitis, town municipalities, and city corporations. Conducted by State Election Commissions, not ECI.

Interim

By-Election (Upchunav)

Held to fill a vacant MP or MLA seat caused by death, disqualification, resignation, or court order. Smaller in scope than a general election.

Executive

Presidential Election

India's President is chosen by an Electoral College of elected MPs and MLAs using the single transferable vote system — not by direct public vote.

06 — Glossary

Key election terms

Essential vocabulary for understanding the Indian election process.

EVM (Electronic Voting Machine)
A standalone, non-networked electronic device used to record votes in India since 2004. Contains a one-time programmable chip. Cannot be hacked remotely. Paired with VVPAT for transparency since 2019.
VVPAT
Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail — a machine attached to the EVM that prints a paper slip showing the voter's chosen candidate for 7 seconds before it drops into a sealed compartment. Provides a physical audit trail.
EPIC (Voter ID Card)
Elector's Photo Identity Card — the official voter identification document issued by ECI. Required at polling booths, though 12 alternate IDs are also accepted.
Electoral Roll
The official list of all registered voters in a constituency. Published and updated by Electoral Registration Officers. You must appear on this list to vote. Verifiable at electoralsearch.in.
First-Past-The-Post (FPTP)
India's voting system — the candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins, even without an absolute majority. A candidate can win with as little as 25–30% of votes if others split the rest.
Model Code of Conduct
A set of guidelines issued by ECI that governs political parties and candidates during elections. Comes into force on the day elections are announced and stays until results are declared.
NOTA (None of the Above)
An option on every Indian EVM since 2013 allowing voters to reject all candidates on the ballot. NOTA votes are counted and announced but do not affect the result — the candidate with the highest non-NOTA votes wins.
Returning Officer
The District Magistrate or senior official designated by ECI to manage the election in one constituency — overseeing nominations, polling, and officially declaring the result.
Hung Parliament
When no single party wins 272+ seats (simple majority in the 543-seat Lok Sabha). Coalition governments are formed through negotiation. India has seen hung parliaments in 1989, 1996, 1998, 1999, and 2009.
Exit Poll
A survey of voters conducted right after they vote. In multi-phase Indian elections, exit polls cannot be published or broadcast until the final phase of polling ends — to avoid influencing later-phase voters.
07 — FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Answers to the most common questions about voting in India.

Who is eligible to vote in India? +
Any Indian citizen aged 18 or above who is registered in the electoral roll of their constituency. NRIs holding Indian passports can register and must vote in person at their home constituency (postal/proxy voting not available for NRIs). People declared of unsound mind by a court, and those imprisoned and serving a sentence are excluded.
Can I vote without a Voter ID card? +
Yes. ECI accepts 12 alternate photo ID documents: Aadhaar card, Passport, Driving Licence, MNREGA job card, Bank/Post Office passbook with photo, Smart Card (ECHS/CGHS), Pension document with photo, and others. However, your name must still appear on the electoral roll — the ID only verifies your identity at the booth.
Are EVMs tamper-proof and reliable? +
EVMs use one-time programmable microcontrollers and are not connected to the internet or any network. They are sealed before and after polling and stored under armed guard. The VVPAT adds a paper verification layer. The Supreme Court has upheld EVM validity in multiple cases, including in 2024, declining to revert to paper ballots.
What if I have moved to a new city? +
You must re-register in your new constituency using Form 6 at voters.eci.gov.in. Also delete your name from the old constituency's roll using Form 7. You can vote only where you are registered, so update before the registration deadline in your new area — typically 60–90 days before the election.
Is my vote really a secret? +
Yes. The secret ballot is a fundamental principle. EVMs do not record which voter pressed which button. The electoral roll serial number given to you is not linked to the EVM unit. Polling booths have screened sections ensuring no one sees your choice. No person can legally compel you to reveal your vote.
What happens if the result is very close? +
Any candidate can request a recount by applying to the Returning Officer before the result is formally declared. Recounts are allowed at the Returning Officer's discretion. Election results can be challenged in the High Court through an Election Petition filed within 45 days. Courts can set aside a result if electoral malpractice is proven.
When is National Voters' Day and why does it matter? +
January 25th — the founding anniversary of the Election Commission of India (established 1950). It is observed every year since 2011 to encourage political participation, especially among first-time voters who turn 18. New voters who registered in the preceding calendar year are felicitated at special events and given their EPIC cards on this day.
